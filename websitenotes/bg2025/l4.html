Bacteria vs phages

Now we're studying a system of two populations: bacteria and phages.
Phages are viruses containing genetic material that attach to a cell and
insert their genetic material into the cell: the cell doesn't recognize
the genetic material inserted as deleterious and so it reproduces it: by
doing so, the cell will become full of phages that eventually will
destroy the cell and the cycle continues. The moment of breaking the
cell is called . Another method is integrating their DNA in the cell
DNA, and so by doing so when the cell reproduce it will produce other
two cells containing the virus DNA. After a while, the virus DNA will be
activated and the cell will start producing viruses that will lead to
lysis. This process is called lysogeny.

Now, we have N bacteria that are not infected and we assume they grow
logistically in absence of viruses but they can be infected by viruses:

\frac{dV}{dt}=\beta\eta I-\phi NV-\omega V $$ where $\beta$ is the **burst size** which is how many viruses are produced after a lysis. Viruses are described by the three parameters: 1. $\phi$ infection rate; 2. $\eta$ rate of lysis, or, contrarily, $\frac{1}{\eta}$ is the time for a lytic event to occur; 3. $\beta$ burst size, meaning the number of viruses produced after a lysis. Parameter $\omega$ is called **dilution** and it's a sort of death rate: imagine we're working in an environment in which we are providing food for the bacteria but we keep removing material that is modeled by $\omega$. Now we make an assumption: infected bacteria don't grow $\left(r_II\left(1-\frac{I+N}{K}\right)\right)=0$ but still drain resources (as they are being used to make viruses) We start by looking for the fixed points. From $\frac{dI}{dt}=0$ we get $I^*=\frac{\phi N^*V^*}{\omega+\eta}$ From $\frac{dV}{dt}=0=V^*\left(\frac{\beta\eta}{\omega+\eta}\phi N^*-\phi N^*-\omega\right)$ There are two options: $V^* \neq 0$ and $V^* = 0$ We call the first one Coexistence fixed point and the second one the Free-virus fixed point ### Coexistence Fixed Point $(N^*, I^*, V^*)$ To find the fixed point where all populations are non-zero, we set the time derivatives to zero: 1. $rN\left(1-\frac{N+I}{k}\right)-\phi NV-\omega N = 0$ 2. $\phi NV-(\eta+\omega) I = 0$ 3. $\beta\eta I-\phi NV-\omega V = 0$ Step 1: Solve for $N^*$ From equation (2), we isolate $I$: $$I^* = \frac{\phi N^* V^*}{\eta + \omega}$$ Substitute this $I^*$ into equation (3): $$\beta\eta \left( \frac{\phi N^* V^*}{\eta + \omega} \right) - \phi N^* V^* - \omega V^* = 0$$ Assuming $V^* \neq 0$, we divide the entire equation by $V^*$: $$\frac{\beta\eta \phi N^*}{\eta + \omega} - \phi N^* - \omega = 0$$ Rearrange to solve for $N^*$: $$N^* \phi \left( \frac{\beta\eta}{\eta + \omega} - 1 \right) = \omega$$ $$N^* = \frac{\omega (\eta + \omega)}{\phi (\beta\eta - \eta - \omega)}$$ Step 2: Solve for $I^*$ From equation (2) to obtain $$I^* = \frac{\phi N^* V^*}{\eta + \omega}$$ Step 3: Solve for $V^*$ From equation (1), assuming $N^* > 0$ : $$r\left(1-\frac{N^*+I^*}{k}\right)-\phi V^*-\omega = 0$$ and using the result of Step 2 we have

r-\omega-r\frac{N^}{k}-\frac{r}{k}\frac{\phi N^ V^}{\eta + \omega} -\phi V^ = 0

r-\omega-r\frac{N^}{k} = \frac{r}{k}\frac{\phi N^ V^}{\eta + \omega} +\phi V^

V^ = \frac{1}{\phi} \left(

--- #### 2. The Explicit Jacobian Matrix The general Jacobian matrix $J$ is given by the partial derivatives of the system with respect to $(N, I, V)$. $$J = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial \dot{N}}{\partial N} & \frac{\partial \dot{N}}{\partial I} & \frac{\partial \dot{N}}{\partial V} \\ \frac{\partial \dot{I}}{\partial N} & \frac{\partial \dot{I}}{\partial I} & \frac{\partial \dot{I}}{\partial V} \\ \frac{\partial \dot{V}}{\partial N} & \frac{\partial \dot{V}}{\partial I} & \frac{\partial \dot{V}}{\partial V} \end{pmatrix}$$ We calculate the terms at the fixed point $(N^*, I^*, V^*)$: 1. **$\dot{N}$ derivatives:** - $\frac{\partial \dot{N}}{\partial N} = r - \frac{2rN}{k} - \frac{rI}{k} - \phi V - \omega$. Simplification: At equilibrium, $\frac{\dot{N}}{N} = r - \frac{rN}{k} - \frac{rI}{k} - \phi V - \omega = 0$. Therefore, the derivative simplifies to $-\frac{rN^*}{k}$. - $\frac{\partial \dot{N}}{\partial I} = -\frac{rN^*}{k}$ - $\frac{\partial \dot{N}}{\partial V} = -\phi N^*$ 2. **$\dot{I}$ derivatives:** - $\frac{\partial \dot{I}}{\partial N} = \phi V^*$ - $\frac{\partial \dot{I}}{\partial I} = -(\eta+\omega)$ - $\frac{\partial \dot{I}}{\partial V} = \phi N^*$ 3. **$\dot{V}$ derivatives:** - $\frac{\partial \dot{V}}{\partial N} = -\phi V^*$ - $\frac{\partial \dot{V}}{\partial I} = \beta\eta$ - $\frac{\partial \dot{V}}{\partial V} = -(\phi N^* + \omega)$ The Explicit Jacobian Matrix: $$J(N^*, V^*) = \begin{pmatrix} -\frac{rN^*}{k} & -\frac{rN^*}{k} & -\phi N^* \\ \phi V^* & -(\eta+\omega) & \phi N^* \\ -\phi V^* & \beta\eta & -(\phi N^* + \omega) \end{pmatrix}$$ Is this fixed point stable? We have to calculate the eigenvalues of this matrix the eigenvalues of a matrix do not change if we do linear combinations of rows columns. Summing first two rows and second and third row we have $$J(N^*, V^*) = \begin{pmatrix} -\frac{rN^*}{k} + \phi V^* & -\frac{rN^*}{k} - (\eta+\omega) & 0 \\ \phi V^* & -(\eta+\omega) & \phi N^* \\ 0 & (\beta-1)\eta - \omega & -\omega \end{pmatrix}$$ Subtracting first from second colum and summing two and third $$J(N^*, V^*) = \begin{pmatrix} -\frac{rN^*}{k} + \phi V^* & -\phi V^* - (\eta+\omega) & 0 \\ \phi V^* & -\phi V^*-(\eta+\omega) & \phi N^* \\ 0 & (\beta-1)\eta - \omega & -\omega \end{pmatrix}$$ To find the Hopf Bifurcation condition (instability of the fixed point), we need to analyze the characteristic equation and apply the Routh-Hurwitz criterion. To make the algebra manageable and the final condition "readable," let's define four natural parameters of the system: 1. **$A = \frac{rN^*}{k}$**: The logistic damping rate of the host. 2. **$T = \phi V^*$**: The transmission rate at equilibrium. 3. **$\delta = \eta + \omega$**: The total decay rate of infected cells. 4. **$B = \eta(\beta-1) - \omega$**: The net viral production rate (burst minus decay). Your Jacobian matrix $J$ can now be written compactly as: $$J = \begin{pmatrix} T - A & -(T + \delta) & 0 \\ T & -(T + \delta) & \phi N^* \\ 0 & B & -\omega \end{pmatrix}$$ The characteristic equation is $\lambda^3 + c_1 \lambda^2 + c_2 \lambda + c_3 = 0$. We calculate the coefficients $c_1, c_2, c_3$ based on the matrix above. Coefficient $c_1$ (Trace): $$c_1 = -\text{Tr}(J) = -( T - A - T - \delta - \omega )$$ Notice the transmission terms $T$ cancel out: $$c_1 = A + \delta + \omega$$ Coefficient $c_2$ (Sum of Principal Minors): This is the sum of the determinants of the $2 \times 2$ diagonal blocks. $$c_2 = M_{11} + M_{22} + M_{33}$$ - $M_{11} = (-\omega)(-T-\delta) - B(\phi N^*) = \omega(T+\delta) - B\phi N^*$ - $M_{22} = -\omega(T-A) = \omega(A-T)$ - $M_{33} = (T-A)(-T-\delta) - T(-(T+\delta)) = A(T+\delta)$ Summing these and simplifying (note that $\omega T$ cancels between $M_{11}$ and $M_{22}$): $$c_2 = A(T + \delta + \omega) + \omega\tau - B\phi N^*$$ Coefficient $c_3$ (Determinant): $$c_3 = -\det(J)$$ $$c_3 = A\omega(T+\delta) + (T-A)B\phi N^*$$ --- #### The Hopf Bifurcation Condition The system undergoes a Hopf Bifurcation (onset of limit cycle oscillations) when: $$c_1 c_2 = c_3$$ Substituting the derived expressions, we get the **explicit condition**: $$\underbrace{(A + \delta + \omega)}_{\text{Damping}} \cdot \underbrace{[ A(T + \delta + \omega) + \omega\tau - B\phi N^* ]}_{\text{Interaction Terms}} = \underbrace{A\omega(T+\delta) + (T-A)B\phi N^*}_{\text{Determinant}}$$ #### Interpretation To verify this condition makes physical sense, we can solve this equality for the Viral Production Parameter $B$ (which contains the burst size $\beta$). This gives us the critical threshold for the bifurcation: $$B_{\text{crit}} = \frac{(A+\delta+\omega)[A(T+\delta+\omega) + \omega\tau] - A\omega(T+\tau)}{\phi N^* (2A + \tau + \omega - T)}$$ - **If $B < B_{\text{crit}}$**: The fixed point is stable (steady coexistence). - **If $B > B_{\text{crit}}$**: The fixed point is unstable, and the system follows a **Limit Cycle**. This shows that increasing the burst size $\beta$ (which increases $B$) is a driver pushing the system into the limit cycle regime. ### the free-phage fixed point $(N^*,0,0)$ This corresponds to $$\begin{split} &A)\;\;N^*=\frac{\omega}{\phi}\left(\frac{\beta\eta}{\omega+\eta}-1\right)^{-1}\\ &B)\;\;V^*=0 \end{split}
